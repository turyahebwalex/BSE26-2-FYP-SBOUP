# Profile Guide — Building a Strong Profile

## Why your profile matters

Your profile is the data the AI uses to calculate your match score for every
job. A thin profile produces low scores across the board. A complete, detailed
profile produces high scores for the right jobs and low scores for irrelevant
ones — which is exactly what you want.

Employers also read your profile before deciding to shortlist you. Think of
it as your permanent CV on the platform.

## Profile completeness breakdown

| Section | Weight | What to add |
|---|---|---|
| Professional title | 15% | Your job title, e.g. "Plumber" or "React Developer" |
| Bio | 10% | 2–4 sentences about your experience and what you offer |
| Location | 10% | Your city or district |
| Skills | 25% | At least one skill with a proficiency level |
| Experience | 25% | At least one past job or project |
| Portfolio | 15% | At least one item — photo, link, or document |

**Total: 100%**

A profile below 60% will score poorly for most opportunities. Aim for 100%.

## How to write a good bio

Your bio should answer three questions:
1. What do you do?
2. How long have you been doing it?
3. What makes you good at it?

**Example (weak):** "I am a developer looking for work."

**Example (strong):** "I am a full-stack developer with 3 years of experience
building web applications using React and Node.js. I have delivered projects
for clients in fintech and e-commerce, and I am comfortable working remotely
or on-site in Kampala."

Keep it under 150 words. Write in first person.

## Adding skills correctly

### Proficiency levels

Choose honestly — employers can tell during interviews.

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Beginner | You understand the basics and can do simple tasks with guidance |
| Intermediate | You can work independently on most tasks |
| Advanced | You handle complex tasks and solve problems without help |
| Expert | You can mentor others and architect solutions |

### How many skills to add

Add every skill you genuinely have. There is no penalty for having many skills.
The AI only counts skills that overlap with a job's requirements — extra skills
do not hurt your score for jobs that do not need them.

### Skill naming tips

Use the same names that appear in job postings:
- "JavaScript" not "JS"
- "Node.js" not "NodeJS" or "Node"
- "UI/UX" not "User Interface Design"
- "Carpentry" not "Wood Work"

## Adding experience

For each experience entry, include:
- Job title
- Employer or client name
- Start and end dates (or mark as current)
- A brief description of what you did

The total months of experience across all entries is used to check if you
meet a job's experience level requirement (entry / mid / senior).

## Adding portfolio items

Portfolio items are the strongest trust signal for employers. Add:
- Photos of completed work (for trade skills)
- Links to live websites or apps (for technical skills)
- Design files or mockups (for creative skills)
- Documents or certificates (for business skills)

Even one portfolio item increases your profile completeness by 15% and
significantly improves employer confidence.

## Expected rate

Set your expected rate range in UGX. This is used to check salary fit:
- If a job's compensation range overlaps with your expected rate → salary fit = yes
- If there is no overlap → salary fit = no, which lowers your match score

Set a realistic range. Too high and you will miss many matches. Too low and
you may be underpaid.

## Keeping your profile updated

Update your profile whenever you:
- Learn a new skill
- Complete a project or job
- Add a portfolio item
- Change your location or availability

The AI re-scores you against all active opportunities every time your profile
changes.
